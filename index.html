Collision trends over time



Steady decrease in injuries and fatalities

From this chart, we observe a clear downward trend in both the number of traffic fatalities and injuries, but why? Perhaps dissecting the temporal patterns of accidents can give us more insight.



Temporal Patterns - Time of Day, Day of Week

The first thing one might notice looking at Collisions Over Time by month, is that there is a noticeable dip during the Spring months every year, sandwiched between the dangerous road conditions of winter and the summertime surge of driving, this appears to be the safest time of year to drive. When we look at collisions by month, spikes in the months from October to January are common at the beginning of the time series, but don't appear to occur in later years. Are these related to weather events? Has cold weather driving technology improved? As you might expect, Friday's tend to see the largest number of accidents; but this difference is perhaps less dramatic amount than you might expect, the number of accidents on a typical Friday only exceeds a the number on a typical Thursday or Wednesday by 10% to 15%. Finally, we can also look at this data and observe that the afternoon rush hour is the time at which the most accidents occur. Is there anything else you notice?





Road Conditions, Driver Age, Driver Sex, Vehicle Age, and Vehicle type



Road Surface Conditions

This chart allows us to see more directly the impact that adverse weather events have had on the number of accidents. It's interesting to note that spikes in the number of accidents occurring under icy/snowy conditions, don't usually translate into a spike in total accidents, but there are exceptions to this rule, such as March 2002, January 2004, and February 2007. When you look at a month like December, it's likely the case that spikes in the number of accidents occurring under icy conditions are the result of icy days displacing dry days.



Driver patterns by age and sex

The general theme of this story board is that men cause more accidents than women. Whether we are talking about young drivers, old drivers, new cars, old cars, light class vehicles, or construction equipment, it's men who tend to cause the most accidents. This chart can also be used to focus our attention on light vehicles, with a potential eye on younger driver. Given the large number of accidents caused by young drivers it's reasonable to further investigate if changes in this demographic might be responsible for decreases in accidents.
